Question
========

*Suppose you were asked to develop a method to predict anaerobic power from a simple field test. Based on the intuition that subjects jumping higher tend to produce larger anaerobic power, you then decide to regress power measured with a reference system against jump height.*
*You then collect data from 20 subjects and obtain the model shown in the figure below.*

:::: {.columns}
::: {.column width=70%}
![](/include/images/part_3/ch_7/power-vs-jumpheight.png)
:::
::: {.column width=30%}
*Although the model explains a substantial proportion of the variability in anaerobic power ($R^2=0.66$), a considerable amount of variability remains unexplained.*
:::
::::
*Which variable from the list below would be the most plausible additional predictor for improving the regression model?*

Answerlist
----------
* *fitness level*
* *body height*
* *body mass*
* *age*
* *heart rate*

Solution
========

The height reached during a vertical jump depends primarily on the vertical velocity of the body at takeoff. Consequently, jump height provides indirect information about the mechanical work and power generated during the movement.

However, two individuals may reach the same jump height while producing different amounts of power. The main reason is that the body mass being accelerated may differ substantially between individuals.

Anaerobic power depends not only on how fast the body is projected upward, but also on the amount of mass that must be accelerated. Therefore, body mass provides additional information that is not fully captured by jump height alone.

For this reason, body mass is the most plausible additional predictor for improving the regression model.
