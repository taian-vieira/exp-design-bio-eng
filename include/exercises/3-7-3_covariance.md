Question
========

*The figure below illustrates two scatterplots, considering body mass as the response variable and height (left plot) and lean body mass (right plot) as predictors.*

*The covariance between body mass and each predictor is shown in the plots.*

![](/include/images/part_3/ch_7/covariance-exercise.png){width=95% fig-align=center}

*Based on the covariance values reported, is it possible to conclude that body mass is more strongly associated with height than with lean body mass?*

Answerlist
----------
* Yes
* No

Solution
=========

Covariance depends on the units of measurement of both variables. Consequently, covariance values cannot be directly compared across relationships involving different predictors.

Although the covariance is larger for body mass and height than for body mass and lean body mass, this does not imply a stronger association.

In fact, the scatterplots suggest the opposite: observations are more closely aligned around a straight line in the right plot than in the left plot. A standardized measure of association is required to compare the strength of the relationships.
