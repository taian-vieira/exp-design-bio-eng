Question
========

##### $\beta_1$ Confidence Interval

*The same conclusion could have been achieved by computing the confidence interval for the population coefficient $\beta_1$*

*Three values for the Student's $t$ are provided below, for different combinations of significance level $\alpha$ and degrees of freedom.*

$$
\begin{aligned}
t_{\nu=2,1-\frac{\alpha}{2}=0.95} &= `r round(qt(0.95,2),3)`, \quad
t_{\nu=17,1-\frac{\alpha}{2}=0.95} = `r round(qt(0.95,17),3)` \\
t_{\nu=2,1-\frac{\alpha}{2}=0.95} &= `r round(qt(0.975,2),3)` \quad
t_{\nu=17,1-\frac{\alpha}{2}=0.95} = `r round(qt(0.975,17),3)`
\end{aligned}
$$

*Based on the appropriate $t$-value, compute the 95% Confidence Interval for $\beta_1$*

$CI_{95\%}\left(\beta_1\right) = ($ ##ANSWER1##, ##ANSWER2## $)$

Answerlist
----------

Solution
========

The 95% confidence interval for $\beta_1$ is defined by:

$$
CI_{95\%}(\beta_1) = \hat{\beta}_1 \pm s_{\hat{\beta}_1} t_{\nu=17,1-\frac{\alpha}{2}=0.97}
$$

Given

$$
\begin{aligned}
\hat{\beta}_1 &= `r round(B[2],1)` \\
s_{\hat{\beta}_1} &= `r round(sqrt(E[2,2]),3)` \\
t_{\nu=17,1-\frac{\alpha}{2}=0.95} &= `r round(qt(0.975,17),3)`
\end{aligned}
$$

we obtain that:

$$
CI_{95\%}(\beta_1) = (`r ci[1]`,`r ci[2]`)
$$
