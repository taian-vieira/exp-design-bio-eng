3  PhyPhox SmartPosture Tutorial

Biomedical Engineering
Applied Statistics

This part of the course provides students with the opportunity to work with their own experimental data. The selected experimental paradigm is postural control. Briefly, postural sway data will be collected under different standing conditions and subsequently used as a benchmark for applying the statistical methods presented later in the course.

As discussed in Chapter 2, stabilometry is considered the gold standard technique for assessing postural sway. Stabilometric analysis is based on changes in the position of the body’s center of pressure (CoP), which are estimated from ground reaction forces.

Collecting stabilometric data during class would be impractical, particularly for a large group of students. Therefore, taking advantage of the widespread availability of smartphones, this course adopts a smartphone-based approach for acquiring postural sway data.

Although students are free to use any application they consider appropriate, all practical activities in this course will rely on the PhyPhox initiative.

… provides a brief tutorial on using PhyPhox to collect postural sway data. Step-by-step instructions are provided for installing the app, running the SmartPosture experiment, and saving and exporting the collected data.


3.1 What is PhyPhox?

PhyPhox is an educational application developed at RWTH Aachen University, Germany. It was designed to enable students to perform scientific experiments using their smartphones, effectively transforming the device into a portable physics laboratory.

PhyPhox stands for physical phone experiments.

With PhyPhox, it is possible to investigate a wide variety of physical phenomena, including:

  • energy dissipated during collisions;
  • centripetal acceleration;
  • the acceleration due to gravity;
  • the frequency and period of a spring oscillator;
  • among many other applications.

PhyPhox operates through experiments, which correspond to predefined sets of instructions governing data acquisition. Users may either select one of the built-in experiments or create custom experiments using the PhyPhox web editor.


3.2 PhyPhox and Postural Sways

For the purposes of this course, a custom PhyPhox experiment—named the SmartPosture Experiment—was developed specifically for measuring postural sway.

The SmartPosture experiment provides an indirect estimate of postural sway based on signals acquired from three smartphone sensors: the accelerometer, gyroscope, and magnetometer. Body sway is estimated from changes in smartphone orientation about its \(x\) and \(y\) axes. According to the reference system illustrated in Figure 3.1, the following convention applies:

  • Sway in the sagittal plane is estimated from smartphone rotation about the \(x\) axis. Thus, pitch reflects the antero-posterior displacement (\(y_{cog}\)) of the body’s center of gravity (CoG).

  • Sway in the frontal plane is estimated from smartphone rotation about the \(y\) axis. Accordingly, roll reflects the medio-lateral displacement (\(x_{cog}\)) of the body CoG.

More specifically, smartphone rotations about the \(x\) and \(y\) axes are estimated using the orientation virtual sensor1.

Rotations in the sagittal plane are represented by \(\theta\) (pitch), whereas rotations in the frontal plane are represented by \(\gamma\) (roll). The coordinates of the body center of gravity (CoG) projected onto the horizontal plane are therefore given by

\[ \begin{aligned} x_{cog}(t) &= \gamma(t) \times h \\ y_{cog}(t) &= -\theta(t) \times h \end{aligned} \tag{3.1}\]

The negative sign ensures that a counterclockwise rotation about the \(x\) axis corresponds to a forward body sway. The parameter \(h\) represents the height of the body CoG above the ground (see Figure 2.2), estimated from the anthropometric data reported by Winter [1]:

\[ h \approx 0.55 \times b_\text{h} \]

where \(b_\text{h}\) denotes total body height.

NoteBeware of the assumptions

Two important assumptions underlie the use of Equation 3.1 to estimate postural sway from smartphone orientation.

First, body rotations are assumed to be sufficiently small for the approximation \(sin \theta \approx \theta\) to remain valid.

Second, the smartphone must be held in portrait orientation, with its \(z\) axis aligned with the direction of gravity (see Figure 3.2).

ImportantValidity of the assumptions

During quiet standing, body movements are typically small enough for both assumptions to be considered valid.

For the educational purposes of this course, the postural sway data estimated from Equation 3.1 provide an appropriate framework for learning the processing of stabilometric data and statistical analysis.

During quiet standing, the arms should hang naturally alongside the body, while the head should remain in a neutral position (neither flexed nor extended).

Although the posture illustrated in Figure 3.2 does not exactly correspond to that adopted during a standard stabilometric assessment, it is presented solely to facilitate data acquisition without requiring assistance from another person.

Hold the smartphone firmly and avoid moving any body segment immediately after starting data acquisition (see the experimental protocol below).


3.3 Installing PhyPhox and the SmartPosture Experiment

To download the PhyPhox app, use the appropriate link below according to the operating system installed on your smartphone.

The next step is to import the SmartPosture experiment into PhyPhox. To do so, follow the four steps illustrated in Figure 3.3 and described below.

Launch the PhyPhox App

When you launch PhyPhox, the home screen displays a list of the built-in experiments available in the application. Because SmartPosture is a custom experiment, it must first be imported. Press the \(+\) icon located in the upper-right corner of the screen (see panel 1 in Figure 3.3).

Import the SmartPosture Experiment

PhyPhox provides three different methods for importing experiments, all illustrated in panel 2 of Figure 3.3. The simplest approach is to scan the QR code shown in panel 3 and reproduced here for convenience.

Save the Experiment to Your Collection

After scanning the QR code, PhyPhox will automatically download and load the SmartPosture experiment.

Because this is the first time the experiment has been loaded, PhyPhox will display a prompt asking whether the remotely hosted experiment should be added to your collection. Select Save to collection so that the experiment becomes permanently available from the home screen.

After the SmartPosture experiment has been successfully added to your collection, it will appear in the PhyPhox home screen and will be ready for use.


3.4 Running the SmartPosture Experiment

This section introduces the basic features of the SmartPosture experiment and explains how to use the application to collect postural sway data.


Launching SmartPosture

After completing the installation procedure described in the previous section, the SmartPosture experiment will appear in your PhyPhox experiment collection.

Launch PhyPhox and open the SmartPosture experiment. It should be listed under the Biomedical tab on the PhyPhox home screen, as illustrated in the figure.


SmartPosture Interface

The SmartPosture experiment comprises two main screens, each providing a different representation of the stabilometric data (see Figure 2.6). These screens are illustrated in Figure 3.4.

Graphs

The Stabilogram screen displays two time-series graphs. The upper graph shows CoG displacement in the medio-lateral (frontal plane) direction, whereas the lower graph displays CoG displacement in the antero-posterior (sagittal plane) direction.

The Statokinesigram screen displays a single graph showing the trajectory of the body CoG projected onto the horizontal plane.

Slider

The slider available on both screens should be adjusted according to the participant’s height, expressed in centimeters. This value is used to scale the smartphone rotations, thereby estimating the horizontal projection of the body CoG according to Equation 3.1.

Dropdown Menus

Two dropdown menus allow the user to specify the base of support and the standing condition adopted during the stabilometric trial. Each menu provides three available options.

The base of support is defined according to foot position during the trial.

  • wide: feet positioned approximately hip-width apart.
  • narrow: feet positioned close together.
  • tandem: one foot positioned directly in front of the other.

The standing condition specifies the experimental condition under which the trial is performed.

  • eyes open: standing quietly with the eyes open.
  • eyes closed: standing quietly with the eyes closed.
  • still: standing while deliberately minimizing postural sway.
Icon Buttons

Three icon buttons are available in the upper-right corner of the screen.

  • Play: starts and stops data acquisition. When pressed, the play icon changes to a pause icon while data are being recorded.

  • Clear: removes all CoG samples currently stored in memory.

  • Settings: opens the configuration menu for the current PhyPhox experiment.


Exporting Data

After completing a stabilometric trial with the SmartPosture experiment, the recorded CoG data can be exported and stored locally on your smartphone. The export procedure is illustrated in Figure 3.5.

After stopping data acquisition, press the Settings button. A menu will appear containing several options, including Export Data. Select this option to open the export menu. Among the available export formats, choose Excel, and then press Export Data in the bottom-right corner of the screen. PhyPhox will then prompt you to choose where the exported file should be saved, either locally on the device or in a cloud storage service.


3.5 The Experimental Protocol

After installing PhyPhox and importing the SmartPosture experiment into your PhyPhox collection, collect postural sway data according to the protocol below.

  1. Position yourself as illustrated in Figure 3.2 while holding the smartphone.

  2. Collect postural sway data using the SmartPosture interface by following the sequence of steps (a to h) described below and illustrated in the accompanying video (Figure 3.6).

  3. Repeat the procedure for each combination of base of support (wide, narrow, and tandem) and standing condition (eyes open, eyes closed, and still).


a: Launch the SmartPosture experiment.

b: Select either the Statokinesigram or Stabilogram visualization.

c: Set your height.

d: Select the appropriate base of support.

e: Select the standing condition.

f: Assume the required posture and press the Play button.

g: Maintain the standing position for 40 s under the selected base of support and standing condition.

h: Export the data in Excel format before performing any other action.

ImportantWhat’s next?

At the end of the experiment, you should have nine Excel files corresponding to the nine combinations of base of support (3) and standing condition (3).

Upload all nine files to the Moodle platform. Instructions for the submission procedure will be provided during class.
